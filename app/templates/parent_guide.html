Parent Guide

A map for parents. The goal isn't to push your child through arithmetic faster — it's to grow a deep, joyful, flexible mathematical mind.

The big idea

Most American math instruction at K–2 emphasizes fluency drills — speed at addition and subtraction. The Soviet/Russian tradition (and most top-performing systems) instead emphasize:

  • Structure — a child should see 7 = 5 + 2 = 4 + 3 = 10 - 3 as the same number wearing different costumes.
  • Reasoning — every problem invites the question "How do you know?".
  • Patterns and invariance — what changes, what stays the same.
  • Multiple representations — fingers, ten-frames, drawings, words, eventually symbols.
  • Strategic games — Nim and Race-to-100 teach more than worksheets.

Your job is not to grade. Your job is to be curious with them.

The four magic prompts

  1. "How do you know?" — every time. Even after a right answer.
  2. "Can you show it another way?" — fingers, drawing, ten-frame, story.
  3. "What if it were different?" — what if it were 8 instead of 7? what if there were 3 baskets?
  4. "What did you notice?" — at the end of a session.

How to keep them motivated

  • Short and frequent beats long. 15 minutes daily > one hour weekly.
  • End on a win. Stop the moment they're proud — not when they're tired.
  • Mark good struggle, not just success. Use the "Good struggle" rating freely. Effort with confusion is the actual learning zone.
  • Praise the strategy, not the speed. "I love that you tried two ways" beats "You're so fast!".
  • Let them be the teacher. Ask them to explain the puzzle to a stuffed animal.
  • Make it physical. Counters, blocks, paper folding, walks counting house numbers.
  • Treat mistakes as discoveries. "Interesting! Let's see why."
  • Keep the door open to depth. If they ask "what about a million?" — go there with them.

What to do when something is "too hard"

  1. Mark "Too hard" — the system steps the strand back next time.
  2. Stop. Don't grind. Switch to something familiar to end on a high.
  3. Try the same idea with smaller numbers ("instead of 13, what if it were 5?").
  4. Try it with concrete objects (cereal pieces, blocks).
  5. Come back to it in two days. Sleep is part of math.

What to do when something is "too easy"

Don't accelerate to multiplication. Go deeper instead.

  1. Ask them to find all the ways: "All ways to make 7."
  2. Ask them to invent a new puzzle of the same type.
  3. Mark "Easy" — system levels up.
  4. Bring in constraints: "Now do it without saying any number above 5."

A weekly rhythm

  • Mon–Fri: 15 min solo session before dinner.
  • One night a week: Math Circle Night — both kids together, more games.
  • Weekend: a kitchen-table puzzle from the bank, or an outdoor counting game.
  • One write-up: a parent note each weekend — what surprised you?

The nine math habits we're building

  1. Subitize — see small groups instantly.
  2. Decompose — split numbers into convenient parts.
  3. Compose — build numbers from parts.
  4. Compare — bigger / smaller / how-many-more.
  5. Reason — give a "because".
  6. Look for pattern — what repeats?
  7. Use multiple representations — picture, story, symbols.
  8. Be systematic — list possibilities in order.
  9. Reflect — what did I learn?

Recommended materials at home

  • 50 small counters (beans, beads, cereal, pennies)
  • 1 deck of playing cards
  • 2 standard dice
  • Wooden blocks or LEGO bricks
  • Tangram set (printable also works)
  • Set of dominoes
  • A printed hundred-chart and ten-frame (one of each is fine)
  • Plenty of paper and a kid-safe pair of scissors

Reading list (for you, the parent)

  • Tracy Zager — Becoming the Math Teacher You Wish You'd Had
  • Jo Boaler — Mathematical Mindsets
  • Alexander Zvonkin — Math from Three to Seven (the Soviet circle classic)
  • James Tanton — Curriculum Inspirations (free, online)
  • Kordemsky — The Moscow Puzzles
